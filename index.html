Why 95% of Product Launches Fail — And What the 5% Do Differently

Vinayak Pandey · Mar 15, 2026 · 8 min read

We've been in the room for over 50 product launches. B2B SaaS, D2C, consumer apps, fintech, enterprise tools. The launches that succeeded and the ones that didn't have one thing in common: the product itself was usually fine. The failure almost always happened upstream.

Most founders build the right product for the wrong customer at the wrong time, and then wonder why it didn't take off.

The Three Failure Modes

After analyzing 50+ launches, we've found that almost every failure maps to one of three root causes: wrong market, wrong customer, or wrong timing. The brutal truth is that most teams don't discover which one it was until they've already spent 12 months and significant capital trying to force it.

1. Building in isolation

The most common failure pattern: a team builds a product for 8–12 months, then takes it to market, and discovers the customer they imagined doesn't actually exist in the way they expected. They did some research — maybe a few user interviews, maybe a survey — but they confused interest signals with purchase intent. People will tell you your idea is great in a conversation. They will not give you their credit card unless the pain is real and urgent.

The fix isn't more research. It's running discovery in parallel with building, and being willing to pivot the scope of the MVP based on what you find. The teams that succeed treat customer discovery as a continuous process, not a one-time kickoff activity.

2. Premature scaling

The second failure mode: scaling marketing and sales before finding product-market fit. A startup raises a seed round, hires a head of marketing, spins up paid acquisition, and tries to grow before the product has demonstrated it can retain users. The result is a leaky bucket — you pour customers in the top and they churn out the bottom faster than you acquire them.

PMF isn't just about activation. It's about retention. Users who activate and then churn haven't found PMF — they found an interesting solution they weren't willing to change their behaviour for. The test is whether, 30–60 days after onboarding, users are still getting value and would be genuinely disappointed if the product disappeared.

3. Copy-paste GTM

The third failure: using the same go-to-market playbook as everyone else in the category. In 2026, most B2B SaaS buyers are numb to generic outbound, tired of gated content, and deeply skeptical of vendor-produced case studies. Running the standard GTM playbook in a competitive category is like wearing the same outfit as everyone else to an interview.

The teams that break through build their GTM around a distinctive point of view, not just a channel. They have something worth saying, and they say it in places where their ICP actually pays attention.

What the 5% Do Differently

The teams that launch successfully share five patterns that are almost universal:

Customer obsession before code. The 5% spend more time with customers before writing a line of code than most teams spend in their entire discovery phase. They're not just validating problem existence — they're mapping the exact job to be done, the existing solution being displaced, the buying decision process, and the economic buyer's incentives.

Ruthless ICP focus. The best launches start by saying no to 90% of potential customers. They pick one specific segment — not "SMBs" but "B2B SaaS CFOs at companies with 50–200 employees who use NetSuite" — and build the first version entirely for that person. Breadth comes after depth, not before.

GTM is built during the build, not after. The most successful teams have their GTM strategy designed and partially tested before the product ships. They know their launch channels, their first 50 customers by name, their outreach sequences, and their pricing before the product is live. By launch day, the marketing machine is running, not starting.

An operator in the room. Not an advisor, not a board member, not a consultant who shows up monthly. An operator who was in the building every week — attending standups, joining sales calls, reviewing the product, and pushing back in real time. This is the Depute model, and it's the single biggest structural difference between teams that execute and teams that plan.

They measure the right things. Vanity metrics — signups, pageviews, app downloads — give teams the feeling of progress without the reality. The 5% instrument their product from day one around the metrics that actually predict PMF: activation rate, day-7 retention, NPS segmented by use case, and time-to-value.

The pattern: The teams that succeed aren't smarter or better funded. They're more rigorous about customer discovery, more focused on a specific ICP, and more disciplined about not scaling until they have real retention signals. The rest is execution.

What to Do With This

If you're pre-launch, audit your customer discovery process. Have you spoken to 30+ people in your ICP this month? Not to pitch — to listen. Do you have a documented jobs-to-be-done for your exact target segment? Do you have 10 people who've said "I'll pay for this on day one"?

If you're post-launch and struggling with traction, run a cohort analysis on your first 60 days of users. Find the 10–15% who are genuinely retained and figure out exactly what job they're hiring your product to do. That's your real ICP. Build everything else around them.

The product launch problem is almost never a product problem.

The Embedded Co-Founder Model: Why Traditional Consulting Fails Founders

Vinayak Pandey · Feb 28, 2026 · 6 min read

Every founder who has hired a consulting firm has a version of the same story. Big kickoff meeting. Lots of enthusiasm. A 60-slide deck arrives six weeks later. The consultants present it, answer questions, and disappear. You're left with a strategy document and the exact same problem you started with — except now you're six weeks and several lakhs of rupees behind.

The consulting model was designed to sell outputs. The founder model requires outcomes. These are fundamentally incompatible.

What's Wrong With Traditional Consulting

They sell deliverables, not outcomes

A consulting engagement is structured around deliverables: a market analysis, a strategy deck, a competitive landscape document, a go-to-market plan. These are measured by their existence and quality, not by whether they produce results. The consultant's job ends when the document is handed over. What happens after is your problem.

This incentive structure means that consultants optimize for the quality of the output, not the probability of success. A beautifully designed deck that nobody executes is a billable success. A messy, imperfect strategy that actually gets implemented is what founders need — but it's not what consulting firms are set up to deliver.

Junior team, senior partner

The economics of consulting firms require that a senior partner sells the engagement while a team of analysts and associates delivers it. You buy based on the partner's experience and reputation. You get the work of people two years out of MBA programs who have never shipped a product. The partner shows up for steering committee meetings.

This matters enormously for product launches, where the most critical work — customer discovery, MVP scoping, positioning — requires pattern recognition from having launched things before. Junior consultants are thorough and process-driven. They are not operators.

No skin in the game

Consulting firms are paid regardless of outcome. Their incentive is to run a clean engagement, avoid conflict, and generate a follow-on project. This means they're unlikely to tell you hard truths — that your product concept is too broad, your target customer is wrong, your pricing model won't work — because those conversations create friction and risk the relationship.

What Embedded Co-Founding Looks Like

The embedded model is structurally different in every dimension that matters.

Accountability for outcomes, not deliverables. An embedded co-founder is compensated partly on milestone achievement — not just on hours billed or documents delivered. If your product doesn't launch, neither of us considers that a success. This alignment changes every decision.

Present in the work, not adjacent to it. Embedded means you're in the Slack channel where decisions are made. You're on the customer call when the buyer says something nobody expected. You're in the sprint review when the team realizes they've built the wrong thing. Being present when things go wrong — and responding in real time — is what creates actual leverage.

Operators, not advisors. The people doing the work at Depute have shipped products. They've done customer discovery interviews, run beta programs, built GTM playbooks that actually got executed. The difference between someone who has built before and someone who has studied how building works is enormous and immediately apparent.

Knowledge transfer is baked in. At the end of a consulting engagement, the knowledge lives in the deck. At the end of an embedded engagement, the knowledge lives in your team — because your team was present for every decision, every customer conversation, and every strategic call. Handover is continuous, not a final ceremony.

When Consulting Actually Works

We're not anti-consulting. There are situations where traditional consulting is exactly the right tool: large-scale operational transformation programs, regulatory compliance work, M&A due diligence. These are problems where having an outside perspective with no operational involvement is a feature, not a bug.

But for product launches, where speed and learning velocity matter more than thoroughness, and where the difference between success and failure is often the quality of a single customer conversation or the courage to kill a feature in week three — you need someone who's in the building with you. Not someone who visits.

The 7 Real Signals of Product-Market Fit That Most Frameworks Miss

Vinayak Pandey · Feb 10, 2026 · 10 min read

The Sean Ellis test — surveying users and checking if 40% would be "very disappointed" if the product went away — is the most widely cited PMF framework in product circles. It's a useful heuristic. But we've seen too many teams obsess over hitting the 40% threshold while missing obvious signs that they don't have fit, and too many teams sit below 40% while actually having a strong, durable business.

PMF isn't a number. It's a collection of signals that point in the same direction. The job is to read all of them.

The 7 Signals We Actually Watch

Signal 1: Users are pulling you toward features you haven't built

When you have PMF, your roadmap backlog is full of feature requests from users who are trying to do more with your product. The conversations shift from "how does this work?" to "can it also do X?" and "when are you building Y?" Users who are pulling you forward have internalized your product as part of how they work. That's a qualitatively different signal than users who activate and go quiet.

Signal 2: Word of mouth happens before you ask for it

One of the clearest PMF signals is when users start referring other people without being prompted. Not because you have a referral program, not because you asked them to — but because the product is solving a real enough problem that they brought it up in conversation with someone else who has the same problem. This unsolicited referral behaviour is a strong predictor of sustainable growth.

Signal 3: Users forgive your bugs

Early-stage products have bugs. Every team knows this. What separates PMF from near-PMF is how users respond to those bugs. Users who have found something genuinely useful will work around problems, report them patiently, and continue using the product while you fix them. Users who haven't found real value will churn at the first friction. If your support tickets read like complaints and churn, that's a signal. If they read like "I know this is broken but here's what I need," that's PMF.

Signal 4: Retention curves flatten — not just at 40%

The classic PMF retention indicator is that your retention curve flattens and holds rather than trending toward zero. A product with PMF retains some cohort of its users at a stable rate — whether that's 20% or 60% depends on the category. What matters is that it stops falling. A product trending toward 0% day-90 retention does not have PMF, regardless of what users say in a survey.

Signal 5: Irrational usage — the product is used for things you didn't design for

When users start using your product for jobs you didn't intend, you've hit something. Notion was designed as a notes tool and users turned it into a CRM, a project manager, a wiki, and a personal operating system. Slack was designed as a team messaging tool and companies started using it as a customer support channel. These aren't misuses — they're signals that the core value proposition is stickier than the specific feature set.

Signal 6: Champions fight internal battles to keep using it

In B2B, one of the strongest PMF signals is when your power users advocate for your product internally — pushing back against IT security reviews, budget freezes, or a manager who wants to consolidate tools. When someone is willing to spend political capital to keep using your product, they have found something genuinely valuable. This is the "pull" that distinguishes real PMF from polite engagement.

Signal 7: You start getting inbound love before you've asked for testimonials

When users proactively send you unsolicited messages — "just wanted to say this changed how we work" — without being prompted, without a request for a testimonial, without a survey — that's a distinct signal. Most teams capture testimonials by asking for them. The teams that have real PMF get them without asking.

How to Read These Signals Together

No single signal is definitive. The power comes from seeing multiple signals pointing in the same direction. If you have strong unsolicited referrals, flat retention at 35%, champions fighting to keep you, and a feature request backlog full of power-user asks — that's PMF even if your Ellis score is 36% and not 40%.

Conversely, if you have a strong Ellis score but zero word of mouth, high churn, and a support queue full of frustrated users — something else is happening. Often it's that your survey methodology is flawed, or users are responding to the idea of the product, not the reality of using it regularly.

The practical test: Pick 10 of your most active users. Ask them what would happen to their workflow if your product disappeared tomorrow. If more than 6 describe a real operational problem — not just mild inconvenience — you're in the zone. If fewer than 3 do, you're still hunting.

GTM Strategy

GTM Design for B2B SaaS in 2026: What's Dead and What Still Works

Vinayak Pandey · Jan 22, 2026 · 9 min read

The playbook that worked in 2021 is largely dead. That was the era of free money, tolerant buyers, and channels that hadn't yet been saturated. Cold email got replies. LinkedIn DMs got meetings. Generic gated content generated qualified leads. Spray-and-pray PPC converted at rational rates.

In 2026, B2B buyers are numb, skeptical, and time-poor. The signal-to-noise ratio in their inbox is near zero. Running the 2021 playbook in today's environment is like putting the same cover letter on every job application and wondering why nobody calls back.

The teams that win in 2026 are the ones that have something worth saying and a specific audience that wants to hear it. Distribution is a by-product of genuine resonance, not a system you bolt on.

What's Actually Dead

Generic cold outbound at scale

The "personalized" cold email that clearly isn't personal, sent from a sequencing tool to a list of 10,000 contacts, generates a fraction of the reply rates it did three years ago. Every buyer knows what these emails look like. The giveaway is the first line: "I noticed you recently [job change / funding round / LinkedIn post]." Buyers are pattern-matching these immediately and deleting them.

What's replaced it: warm outbound with real context. Reaching out to 50 highly specific prospects with a message that references something genuinely specific to their business, their job, and their current situation. Lower volume, far higher signal.

Gated content for lead gen

Gating every piece of content behind a form submission trains your audience to associate your brand with friction. In a world where ungated, high-quality content is abundant, buyers simply find the information they need somewhere else. The teams building the most valuable pipelines are publishing their best thinking openly and getting inbound as a result.

LinkedIn broadcast posting

Volume posting on LinkedIn — three posts per week, engagement-bait hooks, inspirational takes — has been commoditized. Feeds are saturated with this content and buyers scroll past it. What still works on LinkedIn: specific, opinionated, niche content that speaks directly to a highly targeted persona and makes a claim most people in the category would push back on.

What Still Works — and What's Getting Stronger

Problem-led content with genuine expertise

Content that is genuinely useful — not repurposed frameworks, not ChatGPT-generated thought leadership, but hard-won insight from practitioners who have actually done the thing — compounds over time. It attracts inbound from buyers who found it while searching for answers to a real problem. It builds trust before there's any sales conversation. It positions your team as the people worth listening to in the category.

Community seeding and earned distribution

The best B2B SaaS GTMs in 2026 involve building genuine presence in the communities where their ICP spends time — Slack communities, niche newsletters, industry forums, substack comment sections. Not to spam them, but to contribute real value over time. This is a long game, but the conversion rates when it works are dramatically higher than anything from paid channels.

PLG with a sales-assist layer

Product-led growth — letting the product be the primary acquisition and activation vehicle — is working for a broader range of B2B tools than people expected. The key shift is combining PLG with a smart sales-assist layer: not replacing self-serve with salespeople, but identifying which PLG users are expanding in ways that signal enterprise readiness and giving them a human touchpoint at the right moment.

Deep ICP targeting over broad reach

50 perfect prospects, deeply researched and reached with relevant, specific outreach, consistently outperforms 5,000 generic prospects in our experience. The economics feel worse until you measure conversion — at which point the cost per acquired customer is dramatically lower with the targeted approach.

Building Your 2026 GTM

The framework we use when designing GTM for clients starts with one question: where does your ICP go when they have the problem your product solves? Not where do they generally spend time — where do they go specifically when they're looking for answers to the pain you address? That's where your GTM starts.

From there, the rest of the architecture — channels, content strategy, outbound approach, product trials, pricing model — follows from being genuinely useful to that specific person in that specific moment. Everything else is noise.
